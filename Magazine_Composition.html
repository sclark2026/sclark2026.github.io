Composition

Observation

Every aspect of art requires, to some extent, observation of your surroundings. Of all of the fundamentals, I believe that composition is the one that requires the most observational skills and effort.

From Renaissance paintings to photography, composition can be found everywhere. Due to the sheer complexity of this subject, we will be only covering basic compositions. Take note of the rules and try to look for them in everyday life:

  1. The Golden Ratio

    The golden ratio is one of the most renowned compositions of all time. It is naturally-occuring and can even be found in the insides of nautilus shells. The basic guideline to using the golden ratio is to place the points of interest along the line.

  2. Negative Space

    Negative space is used to help direct the viewer's attention to the points of interest. It also helps you avoid visual clutter and noisiness.

  3. Motifs

    Repeating shapes, colors, or values can be used to draw the reader's attention. Patterns can make your illustrations more interesting, especially if they hold symbolic weight.

  4. Symmetry

    It is a frequently used composition and is self-explanatory. Using symmetry can help create either a dynamic or juxtaposition between two or more aspects of your illustration.

  5. Rule of Thirds

    It is similar to the golden ratio, wherein the points of interest are placed within the thirds of the composition. Additionally, you can put the major points of interest on the intersections of the lines.
