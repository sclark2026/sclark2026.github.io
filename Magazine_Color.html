Color Theory

Introduction

Color is an incredibly important aspect of art. It is one of the most freeing fundamentals, such that the creative liberties you can take are infinite. Familiarizing yourself with the basics of color theory, however, is highly recommended prior taking any liberties.

One important aspect to keep in mind, an aspect not limited to colors, is that using references and taking inspiration from other people's art or even from life itself is not, in any way, harming your growth as an artist. In fact, observation is what propels your skills the most. Focus on absorbing as much visual information as you can.

I recommend making a compilation of artwork or artists that use color pallettes or coloring methods that you take a liking to. This album can either be for inspiration or for studying color theory.

Coloring and Rendering Process


Here is an example of a color study I have done in the past.

To walk you through my process: the first thing I did was study my reference image. Secondly, I attempted to emulate the most prominent colors using the color wheel, a process akin to mixing colors in traditional painting. Thirdly, I used a fairly broad brush size to block in shapes of color. When painting, it is often best to start with big rough shapes before getting into the more intricate details.

Lastly, I rendered the drawing. Rendering, in my own definition, is the process of refining and adjusting minute details. It is often the longest and most laborous step of making illustrations. It is only a matter of painting and repainting until you achieve a result you are pleased with. It is very repetitive but just as worthwhile.

This artwork in particular took me approximately three hours and thirty minutes, with three hours spent entirely on rendering. As a sidenote, the longest I've spent on a single illustration is nearly twenty four hours.
